Heatnostics: A Diagnostic Framework for Raster Data

Research Plan — Combining PDF Framework with Implementation Extensions

Abstract: Traditional Scagnostics (Scatterplot Diagnostics) operate on point clouds and fail to capture the grid-based, temporal, and continuous nature of heatmap data. We propose Heatnostics, a domain-specific adaptation that characterizes heatmaps using three geometric pillars: Grid Support (spatial availability), Morse-Smale Topology (signal structure), and EMD Flow (axis transition). This framework extracts a compact feature vector for raster data with varying completeness.

1. Geometric Constraints for Heatmaps

The fundamental difference between scatterplots and heatmaps requires a redefinition of geometric constraints. The following table compares the standard Scagnostics constraints with our proposed Heatnostics constraints:

Feature Standard Scagnostics Proposed Heatnostics Rationale
Vertices Points in ℝ² Centroids of non-null grid cells Heatmaps are discrete samples of a field, not infinite planes
Edges Proximity-based (Delaunay) Adjacency-based (8-connected Moore) Adjacency is intrinsic to grids; Delaunay is irrelevant for lattices
Weights Euclidean distance Gradient-weighted or value-difference Distance on a heatmap is "cost of change", not spatial separation
Direction Undirected Undirected (static) / Directed (temporal) Time implies causality; undirected graphs lose transition info
Nulls N/A (empty space) Topological holes (boundaries) "Null" is a feature (availability), not just emptiness
Manifold Convex plane Discrete surface with genus ≥ 0 Data availability may be fragmented (islands/holes)
Linearity Straight lines Geodesic paths on surface Shortest path between cells must respect the value surface

1.1 Why These Constraints Matter

The "Bad Statistics" Problem: Using Euclidean MST on filtered grid points causes metrics like "Outlying" to be biased toward 100%. This happens because the MST must connect disconnected islands of valid data with massive "super-edges," identifying almost everything as an outlier.

The Fix: Use Geodesic MSF (Minimum Spanning Forest) on the Grid Graph. If two pixels are adjacent and both valid, edge weight = |v₁ - v₂|. If separated by nulls, there is no edge. The result is a Forest (collection of trees), not a single Tree.

2. The Three Geometric Pillars

Heatnostics uses three distinct geometric approaches, each designed to answer a different fundamental question about your data. Think of them as three different "lenses" for examining a heatmap:

I. Spatial Support — "Where is the data?"

Geometry Used: Alpha Shapes + Connected Components

What it measures: This pillar focuses on data availability — not the values themselves, but simply which cells have data and which don't. It treats the heatmap as a binary mask: valid cells vs. holes.

Why Alpha Shapes? An Alpha Shape is a "concave hull" — imagine shrink-wrapping the valid data points. Unlike a convex hull (which draws a rubber band around all points), an alpha shape follows the actual boundary of the data, including concave indentations. This lets us accurately measure holes inside the data region, not just empty space around it.

Real-world analogy: Imagine looking at a road survey map. Some segments were surveyed, some weren't. Spatial Support tells you: "What percentage of the highway was actually measured? Are the gaps scattered randomly or concentrated in one area? Is the data fragmented into disconnected islands?"

Key metrics: Porosity (hole ratio), Fragmentation (island count), Diagonal Connectivity (connection pattern)

II. Signal Topology — "What patterns exist in the values?"

Geometry Used: Morse-Smale Complex + Statistical Moments

What it measures: This pillar focuses on the actual values in the heatmap — the "terrain" of the data. It finds local peaks (high spots), valleys (low spots), and the ridges/valleys connecting them.

Why Morse-Smale? Morse theory treats your heatmap as a 3D terrain where the Z-axis is the cell value. Just like a topographic map has mountain peaks, valley floors, and saddle points (passes between mountains), your heatmap has critical points. The Morse-Smale complex partitions the entire heatmap into regions that "flow" to the same peak and valley pairs.

Real-world analogy: For highway condition data, a "peak" is a segment in excellent condition, a "valley" is a damaged segment. The number of peaks tells you if you have many scattered good spots or one large good region. The skewness (Distress Intensity) tells you if your road is "mostly good with a few potholes" or "uniformly mediocre."

Key metrics: Peaks/Valleys (critical point count), Distress Intensity (skewness), Error Noise (oscillation detection)

III. Axis Transition — "How does data change along one axis?"

Geometry Used: Earth Mover's Distance (Optimal Transport)

What it measures: This pillar focuses on transitions along one axis of the heatmap. It asks: "How much does the data distribution change as we move along axis A? Is there a directional trend?" This works for both time-based and variable-based heatmaps.

Why Earth Mover's Distance? EMD (also called Wasserstein distance) measures the minimum "work" needed to transform one distribution into another. Imagine each row (or column) as a pile of dirt. EMD asks: "How much dirt do I need to move to reshape row N into row N+1?" This captures both the magnitude of change (flux) and the direction of change (drift).

For time axis: Low flux = stable over years. Positive drift = deteriorating. Negative drift = improving.

For variable axis: Low flux = smooth gradient along the variable. High flux = abrupt changes. Drift indicates whether values tend to increase or decrease along that axis.

Key metrics: Transition Flux (change magnitude), Drift (change direction)

3. Metric Definitions

3.1 Spatial Support Metrics

These metrics measure data availability — where data exists, what's missing, and how coverage is structured.

Porosity [Implemented]

Porosity = Hole_Area / Alpha_Shape_Area

Measures the fraction of missing data inside the concave boundary of valid cells. Unlike simple sparsity, porosity only counts holes within the data region, not empty space around it.

  • Range: 0 to 1
  • 0 = Full grid, no holes
  • 0.3 = 30% "Swiss cheese" pattern
  • Use case: Detect missing survey coverage

Fragmentation [Implemented]

Fragmentation = Count of 8-connected components

Counts how many disconnected islands of valid data exist using 8-connected flood fill (including diagonal neighbors).

  • Range: 1 to N
  • 1 = Single connected region
  • High = Scattered islands of data
  • Use case: Identify severe data gaps

Diagonal Connectivity [Implemented — Extension]

DiagonalConn = (Components_4connected - Components_8connected) / Components_4connected

Measures reliance on diagonal-only connections by comparing 4-connectivity vs 8-connectivity. Detects "checkerboard" gap patterns that row-wise methods miss.

  • Range: 0 to 1
  • 0 = Well-connected (no diagonal reliance)
  • High = Checkerboard pattern, sparse grid
  • Use case: Irregular/sparse raster grids

Solidity [Next Phase]

Solidity = Valid_Pixels / Convex_Hull_Area

Ratio of actual valid pixels to convex hull area. Low solidity indicates irregular boundary with concave regions.

Tortuosity [Next Phase]

Tortuosity = Perimeter² / (4π × Area)

Measures boundary complexity. A circle has tortuosity = 1. Higher values indicate fractal-like coastlines.

3.2 Signal Topology Metrics

These metrics measure value distribution — patterns, local extrema, and statistical properties.

Peaks & Valleys (Morse-Smale) [Implemented]

Peaks = Count of local maxima (all neighbors have lower values)
Valleys = Count of local minima (all neighbors have higher values)

Identifies critical points in the value surface using Morse-Smale theory. For highway data: peaks = good condition areas, valleys = damage hotspots.

  • Many peaks = Multiple distinct hotspots
  • Few peaks = Smooth, uniform surface
  • Use case: Find localized problem areas

Distress Intensity [Implemented — Extension]

Distress Intensity = Skewness(all_scores) = E[(X - μ)³] / σ³

Measures asymmetry of score distribution. Differentiates "uniformly mediocre" roads from "mostly good with severe potholes."

  • Negative skew = Tail towards low scores (damage hotspots)
  • Positive skew = Tail towards high scores (isolated good spots)
  • Zero = Symmetric distribution
  • Use case: Characterize damage patterns

Error Noise [Implemented — Extension]

Error Noise = Oscillations / (N - 2)
where Oscillation = sign change in (v[i] - v[i-1]) vs (v[i+1] - v[i])

Measures high-frequency oscillations. Counts how often values alternate up-down-up along each row. High rates suggest sensor errors rather than real physical changes.

  • Range: 0 to 1
  • 0 = Smooth, monotonic data
  • 1 = Maximum oscillation (pure noise)
  • Use case: Flag suspicious data quality

Roughness [Next Phase]

Roughness = Surface_Area_3D / Footprint_Area_2D

Treats heatmap as 3D surface. High roughness = volatile local changes.

3.3 Axis Transition Metrics

These metrics measure distribution changes along one axis. The framework supports two directions:

  • Row-to-row: Compare distribution of each row to the next
  • Column-to-column: Compare distribution of each column to the next
Why no diagonal transitions?

Unlike spatial connectivity (which uses 8-connected neighbors for adjacency), EMD compares entire row/column distributions — not individual cell pairs. Diagonal transitions are excluded for three reasons:
  1. Unclear semantics: Diagonal mixes two axes simultaneously (e.g., "time AND position"), making interpretation confusing.
  2. Redundant information: Diagonal transitions decompose into row + column transitions. Measuring them separately provides cleaner, more interpretable results.
  3. Anisotropic data: When axes represent different types (time vs. space, directed vs. undirected), mixing them in a diagonal obscures the distinct dynamics of each axis.
For isotropic data (both axes same type, e.g., lat × lon), diagonal analysis could detect directional texture patterns — but this is a specialized extension, not part of the core framework.

Transition Flux [Partial — Row only]

Flux = Average Wasserstein distance between consecutive rows (or columns)
W₁(P, Q) = ∫|CDF_P(x) - CDF_Q(x)|dx

Uses Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) to measure how much "work" is needed to transform one row's (or column's) distribution into the next.

  • Low flux = Stable/smooth transitions along the axis
  • High flux = Volatile, abrupt changes between consecutive slices
  • Row direction (time axis): Measures year-to-year stability
  • Column direction (position axis): Measures spatial gradient smoothness

Drift [Partial — Row only]

Drift = Signed CDF difference = ∫(CDF_P(x) - CDF_Q(x))dx

Measures directional bias of change along an axis. Unlike flux (magnitude only), drift tells you which direction values are shifting.

  • Positive drift = Distribution shifting toward lower values
  • Negative drift = Distribution shifting toward higher values
  • Zero = Balanced (no net directional change)
  • Row direction (time axis): Positive = deteriorating, Negative = improving
  • Column direction: Indicates spatial trend along that axis

Coherence [Future]

Coherence = |∑V| / ∑|V| where V = gradient vectors

Measures whether gradient vectors point in same direction (laminar flow) or random (turbulent). 1 = coherent; 0 = chaotic.

Causal Entropy [Future]

Causal Entropy = H(Y_t+1 | Y_t)

Conditional entropy measuring how predictable the next time step is. Low = predictable; High = chaotic.

7. Related Work

This section surveys the research landscape that informs and motivates Heatnostics. We organize our review into six threads: (1) scagnostics and its extensions, (2) topological data analysis for scalar fields, (3) optimal transport and distribution comparison, (4) texture analysis in image processing, (5) infrastructure condition monitoring, and (6) handling missing data in raster visualization. We conclude by positioning Heatnostics at the intersection of these threads, addressing gaps that none individually fills.

7.1 Scagnostics: Origins and Extensions

The term "scagnostics" (scatterplot diagnostics) was coined by John and Paul Tukey in 1985, though a formal computational framework was not developed until Wilkinson, Anand, and Grossman's 2005 IEEE InfoVis paper [1]. Their approach constructs geometric graphs—Delaunay triangulation, Minimum Spanning Tree (MST), and alpha hulls—from scatterplot point coordinates, then extracts nine metrics: Outlying, Skewed, Clumpy, Sparse, Striated, Convex, Skinny, Stringy, and Monotonic. These metrics enable automated navigation of large scatterplot matrices, guiding analysts toward "interesting" variable pairs without exhaustive manual inspection.

Extensions and Applications. Since 2005, scagnostics have been extended in several directions. Dang et al. [2] introduced ScagExplorer, an interactive system for exploring high-dimensional data using scagnostics as a lens for dimension reduction. Lehmann et al. [3] extended scagnostics to 3D point clouds, developing nD scagnostics that characterize data in higher-dimensional spaces—useful for guiding clustering or outlier detection in spaces beyond visualization limits. Recent work has integrated scagnostics with deep learning: convolutional neural networks can now estimate scagnostics scores from scatterplot images, enabling real-time classification at scale [4]. The ScagnosticsJS library provides browser-based implementations supporting 2D, 3D, and higher-dimensional computations [3].

Limitations for Heatmaps. Despite their utility, scagnostics are fundamentally designed for point clouds in continuous Euclidean space. Three assumptions fail when applied to raster heatmaps:

  • Euclidean proximity: Scagnostics use Euclidean distance for graph construction, but heatmap cells have discrete lattice adjacency—a cell is "near" its 4 or 8 neighbors, regardless of value.
  • Completeness: Scagnostics assume all data points exist. Heatmaps often have systematic gaps (null cells) that carry semantic meaning—a sensor was offline, a survey was incomplete.
  • Static analysis: Standard scagnostics treat data as a single snapshot. Temporal heatmaps (rows = time) require transition-aware metrics.

Heatnostics addresses these gaps by replacing Euclidean primitives with grid-native structures, treating nulls as topological features, and introducing axis transition metrics.

7.2 Topological Data Analysis and Scalar Field Visualization

Topological Data Analysis (TDA) provides mathematical tools for characterizing the "shape" of data. For scalar fields—functions assigning values to points in space—Morse theory [5] serves as the foundational framework. Morse theory analyzes a field through its critical points: locations where the gradient vanishes (maxima, minima, saddles). The topology of level sets changes only at critical points, making them natural features for segmentation and summarization.

Morse-Smale Complexes. The Morse-Smale (MS) complex partitions a domain into regions sharing common gradient flow behavior—cells connected to the same maximum and minimum [6]. Edelsbrunner et al. [7] formalized hierarchical MS complexes for piecewise-linear 2-manifolds. Gyulassy et al. [8] developed efficient algorithms for computing MS complexes on 3D scalar fields, enabling applications in combustion simulation [9] and climate modeling. Discrete Morse theory, introduced by Forman [10], extends these concepts to combinatorial structures like meshes and grids, making them applicable to digital images.

Persistence and Robustness. Persistence diagrams [11] quantify the "lifetime" of topological features across scales, filtering noise from significant structure. A peak that persists across many threshold levels is more significant than one that appears and disappears quickly. This robustness to localized noise is essential for real-world data with measurement errors.

Applications in Visualization. TDA has been applied to terrain analysis (watershed segmentation), medical imaging (tumor detection), materials science (pore structure analysis), and astrophysics (cosmic web characterization). Our Peaks and Valleys metrics adapt critical point counting for discrete heatmaps, while future work on Roughness and persistence-based filtering will further leverage TDA concepts.

7.3 Optimal Transport and Distribution Comparison

Optimal Transport (OT) theory, originating with Monge (1781) and formalized by Kantorovich (1942), provides a principled framework for comparing probability distributions. The Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), also known as the 1-Wasserstein distance, measures the minimum "work" (mass × distance) required to transform one distribution into another [12]. Unlike divergences (KL, JS), EMD is a true metric satisfying the triangle inequality, and it provides an interpretable transport plan—which parts of the source distribution map to which parts of the target.

Applications. Rubner et al. [13] pioneered EMD for image retrieval, showing it outperformed histogram intersection for matching perceptually similar images. In machine learning, OT underpins domain adaptation [14] and generative modeling (Wasserstein GANs). Peyré and Cuturi [15] provide a comprehensive survey of computational optimal transport, including entropic regularization for scalable approximations.

Temporal Change Detection. For time series and temporal data, OT-based methods detect "change points" where the underlying distribution shifts significantly [16]. This non-parametric approach makes no assumptions about distribution shape, detecting both abrupt transitions and gradual drifts. Applications include financial anomaly detection, ECG monitoring, and human activity recognition.

Heatnostics Application. Our Transition Flux metric computes EMD between consecutive rows (or columns) of a heatmap, capturing global redistribution patterns across the axis. The Drift metric—signed CDF integral—adds directionality, distinguishing improvement from deterioration in infrastructure contexts.

7.4 Texture Analysis in Image Processing

Texture analysis characterizes spatial patterns in images beyond simple statistics. Haralick et al. [17] introduced the Gray-Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM), computing joint probabilities of pixel intensity pairs at specified offsets. From GLCM, features like Contrast, Correlation, Energy (uniformity), and Homogeneity describe whether texture is smooth, structured, or chaotic. GLCM has been widely adopted in remote sensing (land use classification), medical imaging (tumor grading), and materials science (surface quality).

Minkowski Functionals. For binary or thresholded images, Minkowski functionals—area, perimeter, and Euler characteristic—provide morphological descriptors rooted in integral geometry [18]. These have been applied to cosmology (galaxy distribution), porous media analysis, and defect detection in manufacturing.

Limitations for Sparse Data. Both GLCM and Minkowski functionals assume complete images. When applied to sparse grids with missing data, they either fail (undefined co-occurrences) or produce biased estimates (treating nulls as a "color"). Heatnostics adapts these concepts for sparse contexts: Porosity quantifies holes using alpha shapes rather than pixel counting; planned Contrast metrics will compute GLCM only over valid cell pairs.

7.5 Infrastructure Condition Monitoring

Pavement management systems (PMS) form a critical application domain for Heatnostics. Road agencies worldwide collect condition data—Pavement Condition Index (PCI), International Roughness Index (IRI), distress surveys— organized as spatial (location along highway) and temporal (year of survey) grids [19]. Deterioration modeling predicts future conditions using Markov chains, survival analysis, or machine learning [20].

Data Quality Challenges. Real pavement data exhibits systematic sparsity: segments are surveyed on rotating schedules, sensors fail, weather disrupts collection. Traditional analyses impute missing values or exclude incomplete records, losing information about the missingness pattern itself. For agencies, knowing where data is missing—and whether gaps are random or systematic—is as important as the condition scores themselves.

Spatial-Temporal Analysis. Geographic Information Systems (GIS) enable spatial visualization and analysis of pavement data [21]. Machine learning approaches increasingly incorporate spatial structure, recognizing that adjacent segments deteriorate similarly due to shared traffic and climate [22]. Graph Neural Networks have been applied to road networks, propagating information along connectivity structure.

Heatnostics Contribution. For infrastructure monitoring, Heatnostics provides domain-agnostic summary statistics that complement traditional condition indices. Porosity quantifies survey completeness; Fragmentation identifies disconnected monitored regions; Drift tracks whether conditions are improving or deteriorating systemically, not just locally.

7.6 Missing Data in Raster Visualization

Missing data (NoData cells) in raster datasets arises from sensor failures, processing errors, cloud cover in satellite imagery, or intentional survey design [23]. GIS tools provide imputation methods—inverse distance weighting, kriging, neighbor-based filling—that estimate missing values from surrounding cells. These are appropriate when the goal is producing complete surfaces for analysis, but they obscure information about the spatial pattern of missingness [24].

Missing Data as Information. In some contexts, the pattern of missingness carries semantic meaning. In infrastructure monitoring, a gap may indicate construction (section closed for work), administrative boundaries (different agency jurisdictions), or systematic data collection failures (equipment deployed only in certain weather). Imputing these gaps erases this information; visualizing them as "holes" preserves it.

Heatnostics Approach. Rather than imputing missing values, Heatnostics treats nulls as first-class topological features. The alpha shape bounds valid data; holes within this boundary are counted and measured. Porosity, Fragmentation, and Diagonal Connectivity all characterize the structure of availability, not just the presence of gaps. This aligns with cartographic best practices for uncertainty visualization [25] and enables downstream analysis to account for data provenance.
